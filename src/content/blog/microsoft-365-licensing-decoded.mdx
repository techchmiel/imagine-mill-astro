Microsoft 365 licensing has a reputation, and the reputation is deserved. Plans with near-identical names, features that moved tiers three renames ago, and a comparison chart that requires a second monitor and a support group.

But underneath the sprawl, the structure is actually sensible — once you know the two questions it's organized around: **how big are you**, and **how much security and compliance do you need built in**. Let's decode it top to bottom.

## The map: three families

**Business family — for organizations up to 300 seats.** Business Basic, Business Standard, and Business Premium. The 300 is a real, enforced ceiling across the whole family combined — grow past it and you're shopping in the enterprise aisle whether you like it or not.

**Enterprise family — unlimited seats, deeper toolbox.** Office 365 E1/E3/E5 (the apps and services alone) and Microsoft 365 E3/E5 (the same, bundled with Windows enterprise licensing and the security/management stack). When people argue about "E3 vs. E5," they usually mean the Microsoft 365 flavor.

**Frontline family — F1/F3.** Built (and priced) for shift, retail, and field workers who mainly need mobile/web access, Teams, and communication rather than a desktop Office suite on a dedicated computer.

## The Business tiers, in one breath each

- **Business Basic:** web and mobile only. Email, Teams, OneDrive, SharePoint, browser versions of Word/Excel/PowerPoint. No installed desktop apps. Great for lean teams; a false economy for anyone living in Excel.
- **Business Standard:** Basic plus the full installed desktop apps. The default choice for most small businesses.
- **Business Premium:** Standard plus the security stack — device management (Intune), advanced email/threat protection (Defender flavors), and **Entra ID P1**, which unlocks [Conditional Access](/articles/conditional-access-for-normal-humans/). For any small org that handles money or client data — which is all of them — this tier is where "we take security seriously" stops being a slide and starts being infrastructure.

<Callout type="tip" title="The small-business cheat code">
Business Premium is quietly one of the best bundles Microsoft sells: a meaningful slice of the enterprise security stack at small-business pricing. If you're under 300 seats and comparing it against E3, Premium wins more often than the naming implies.
</Callout>

## E3 vs. E5: the actual difference

**Microsoft 365 E3** is the enterprise workhorse: everything installed, unlimited seats, Windows enterprise, device management, solid baseline compliance. Most large organizations run mostly-E3.

**Microsoft 365 E5** is E3 plus the premium security and analytics estate: advanced threat protection across endpoints and email, risk-based identity protection (Entra ID P2), the fuller Purview compliance suite (advanced eDiscovery, insider risk), Power BI Pro, and Teams Phone capabilities. 

The honest test for E5 isn't "is it better?" — it is, and it costs like it. The test is: **would we otherwise buy these capabilities separately?** An org running a third-party SIEM-lite, a separate phone system, standalone BI licenses, and an eDiscovery tool may find E5 *cheaper* than its pile of point solutions. An org that would use none of those is buying a very nice gym membership for the January version of itself.

Middle paths exist and are underused: Microsoft sells **E5 Security** and **E5 Compliance** as add-ons to E3, and you can mix tiers per population — E5 for IT, security, legal, and executives (the accounts attackers actually target), E3 for everyone else. Licensing is per-user; uniformity is a habit, not a requirement.

## The questions that pick your answer

1. **Over 300 people, or will be soon?** Enterprise family. Done.
2. **Under 300?** Start at Business Standard; move to Premium the moment you care about device management, Conditional Access, or phishing beyond the basics. (You care. See question 3.)
3. **Handle client data, money, or regulated anything?** Then the security tier isn't optional — Business Premium or E3-with-security-add-ons is your floor.
4. **Paying separately for phone systems, BI, or compliance tooling?** Price E5 against the pile before renewing either.
5. **Lots of frontline staff?** Don't buy desk-worker licenses for people without desks — that's what F plans are for.

And one meta-rule: **audit what you're already paying for before buying anything new.** Nearly every tenant contains a few surprises — licensed accounts of people who left, E5 features nobody turned on, [Entra ID P1 sitting unused in Business Premium](/articles/conditional-access-for-normal-humans/) like airbags still in the trunk. The cheapest license is the one you already own and finally switch on.

## About prices (deliberately absent)

You'll notice no dollar figures here. Prices change, vary by region and agreement, and any number we print becomes a small landmine within a year — check the current price list or your reseller quote. The *structure* above is what stays stable, and structure is what actually decides the choice. (One recent structural note: [Copilot is an add-on](/articles/rolling-out-copilot-without-the-chaos/) on top of nearly all of these plans, so it changes budgets without changing this map.)

## The takeaway

Size picks your family. Security appetite picks your tier. E5 is a consolidation play, not a status symbol. Frontline workers get frontline plans. And somewhere in your tenant right now, there is almost certainly a feature you're paying for that would solve a problem you're also paying to solve a second way — which makes a license audit the rare meeting that funds itself.
