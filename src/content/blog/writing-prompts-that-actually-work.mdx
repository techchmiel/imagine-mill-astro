Two colleagues get Copilot licenses the same day. A month later, one calls it "a glorified autocomplete" and the other quietly refuses to work without it. Same tool, same data, same job. The difference is almost always in the six seconds before they hit enter.

Prompting isn't a dark art and it isn't "engineering," whatever the job listings say. It's a communication skill you already have — you use it every time you delegate work to a person. You've just been forgetting to use it on the machine.

## The intern test

Here's the frame that fixes most bad prompts instantly: **Copilot is a brilliant intern on their first day.** Deeply capable, impossibly fast, has read everything your account can open — and knows absolutely nothing about what *you* actually want, who the audience is, or what "make it good" means on your team.

You'd never tell a first-day intern "write something about the project." You'd say: what document, for whom, covering what, at what length, by when. That gap — between what you'd tell a person and what you type into the chat box — is where almost all AI disappointment lives.

## The four ingredients

Every strong workplace prompt contains some mix of four things. (Microsoft's own guidance uses a similar recipe — goal, context, expectations, source — this is that idea in plain clothes.)

**1. The goal — what do you want, concretely?**
Weak: "Summarize this document."
Strong: "Summarize this document in five bullet points focused on the decisions made and who owns each next step."

**2. The context — who's it for, and why?**
Weak: "Write an email about the outage."
Strong: "Write an email to non-technical department heads explaining yesterday's outage: what happened, impact, what we've fixed, apologetic but not groveling."

**3. The format — what shape should the answer take?**
Table, bullets, one paragraph, a draft agenda, an Excel formula. If you don't say, you get the model's favorite shape, which is "confident medium-length essay."

**4. The source — what should it work from?**
This is the superpower specific to [Microsoft 365 Copilot](/articles/rolling-out-copilot-without-the-chaos/): it can reference your actual files, meetings, and mail. Point it: "based on the Q3 planning doc and last Tuesday's steering meeting…" A prompt with a named source beats a prompt with a vague memory, every time.

## The habit nobody discovers on their own

Here's the single biggest behavior gap between the "useless" camp and the "can't work without it" camp: **the first answer is the first draft of a conversation, not the verdict.**

When a human hands you a draft that's 70% right, you don't declare the human broken — you say "tighter, and lose the second paragraph." Copilot takes exactly that kind of direction:

- "Shorter. Half the length."
- "Make the tone warmer — this reads like a legal notice."
- "Turn the middle section into a table."
- "Now write the skeptical-CFO version."

Each refinement takes five seconds. People who iterate twice get output that people who prompt once swear is impossible.

## Three prompts to steal this week

1. **The meeting rescue:** "Summarize the decisions and action items from [meeting], grouped by owner. Flag anything with no owner." — the follow-up email writes itself, minus the hour.
2. **The inbox triage:** "Review my unread email from the last three days. List anything that needs a reply from me, most urgent first, one line each." — Monday morning, tamed.
3. **The blank-page breaker:** "Draft an outline for [document] aimed at [audience], covering [topics]. Rough is fine — I'll rewrite." That last clause is oddly liberating for both parties.

## The honesty section

Two disciplines keep all this from going sideways:

**Verify like it's an intern's work — because it is.** Copilot can be confidently wrong: a date misread, a number from the *old* version of the file, a plausible-sounding claim stitched from thin air. The rule that scales: the more consequential the output, the more carefully a human reads it before it ships. First drafts, always; final drafts, never unattended.

**Don't paste in what shouldn't travel.** Inside Microsoft 365 Copilot, your prompts stay within your organization's boundary and it can only see what your account can see. But the habit matters beyond work: the same sentence typed into a random free chatbot is a disclosure. Know which window you're typing into — and if your org hasn't sorted [who can see what](/articles/rolling-out-copilot-without-the-chaos/), prompting skill is the least of its problems.

## The takeaway

Goal, context, format, source — then iterate like you're managing a fast, eager, slightly overconfident new hire. That's the entire skill. No certification required, despite what your LinkedIn feed suggests.

And the next time someone declares the tool useless, glance at their prompt. It will say something like "make this better." Somewhere, an intern who was never told what "better" means nods in solidarity.
