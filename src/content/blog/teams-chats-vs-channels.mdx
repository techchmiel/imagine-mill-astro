Every Teams tenant contains a group chat named something like "Project Falcon (NEW)" — eleven members, four of whom left the project, one of whom left the company, and somewhere in its 3,000-message scroll is the only record of a decision worth thousands.

Nobody chose this. It just happened, one "quick chat" at a time. Let's un-happen it.

## The rule

> **Chats are for people. Channels are for work.**

A chat is a conversation between specific humans. It follows the people, not the topic: your 1:1 with a colleague drifts across projects and years, and that's fine — it's yours.

A channel is a place where a *topic* gets worked on. People come and go; the conversation stays put, searchable, joined to its files, visible to whoever joins next month.

The failure mode is always the same: **work happening in chats**. It feels faster — and it is, for about two weeks. Then someone new joins and can't see any history (new members of a group chat can't scroll back to what happened before them, while channels show everything). Someone leaves, taking context with them. And the decision your auditor is asking about lives in a container named "Quick sync — Falcon stuff."

## The decision map

| The conversation is… | It belongs in… |
| --- | --- |
| "Lunch?" / anything you'd say at their desk | 1:1 chat |
| Personal, sensitive, or about a person | 1:1 chat |
| Quick logistics among 3 people, dead by Friday | Group chat |
| Anything about a project with a lifespan | Channel |
| A decision someone might need in six months | Channel |
| An announcement people will need to find again | Channel |
| "Where's the latest version of…" | Channel — and the file's right there in its Files tab |

Two ways to smell a container going wrong:

- **A group chat that keeps growing.** If you're adding a sixth person, or renaming it, or someone writes "we should really keep this together" — that's a channel introducing itself. Create one, post a link in the chat, move on with your life.
- **A channel used like a chat.** Fourteen messages that could have been one thread, none using reply. Channels work when conversations use threads — one topic, one thread, replies inside it — so that six months later the whole decision reads top to bottom in one place.

<Callout type="tip" title="The six-month test">
Before you hit send, one question: might anyone need this in six months? Then it goes in a channel, in a thread, with a subject line. Future You is the main beneficiary, and Future You has forgotten everything.
</Callout>

## Files follow the container (this is the part people miss)

Here's the mechanical difference that makes the rule matter more than etiquette:

- A file shared in a **chat** lives in the sender's **OneDrive**, shared to those specific people.
- A file shared in a **channel** lives in the team's **SharePoint library**, owned by the team.

Which means "just send it in the chat" quietly routes team documents into personal storage — the exact [OneDrive-vs-SharePoint mistake](/articles/sharepoint-or-onedrive-a-decision-map/) that surfaces when someone resigns. The container you pick for the *conversation* silently picks the home for the *files*. Choose accordingly.

## "But nobody reads the channels"

The most common objection, and it's fair — because most channel sprawl is self-inflicted. A team with nineteen channels named after moods will train everyone to ignore all of them.

The fixes are boring and they work:

1. **Fewer channels, clearer names.** Start with General plus two or three that map to actual workstreams. Add more only when a thread population demands it.
2. **Use threads with subject lines.** A channel post can have a bold title. Posts with titles get read; walls of loose messages don't.
3. **@mention with intent.** Mention a person when you need *them*; mention the channel only for genuine everyone-news. Cry-wolf mentions are why notifications get muted, and muted channels are why "nobody reads the channels."
4. **Close chats that graduated.** When a group chat becomes a channel, actually say so in the chat and stop posting there. Two homes for one topic is worse than either.

## The honest summary

Chats are hallway conversations: fast, human, ephemeral — and that's their job. Channels are the meeting room with a whiteboard nobody erases: slower to love, but everything sticks, and anyone who joins can read the walls.

Put people-talk in chats. Put work in channels. And when in doubt, ask whether the message deserves to outlive the week — because in six months, someone will be searching for it, and the only question is whether they'll find a thread or a tombstone named "Project Falcon (NEW)."
